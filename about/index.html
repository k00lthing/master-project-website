About the Project

Idea

This project started with a contemplating about readability, legibility, and its role in type design. The theory goes as follows: legibility is the ease with which a reader can decode individual symbols, while readably is the ease with which a reader can follow and understand words, sentences, and paragraphs. Traditionally, type design practitioners strive to construct their typefaces to be legible and readable in all different situations and applications, which is a long and dire process involving lots of compromises. But who tells what is legible and/or readable? And what is legible and/or readable for whom?

The technology of optical character recognition (OCR) has become a ubiquitous and essential part of many applications. I, personally, used it for a long time without giving it much though, until I watched David Kriesel’s 2014 CCC congress talk “Don't trust any scan that you haven't faked yourself” (originally in German “Traue keinem Scan, den du nicht selbst gefälscht hast“). The case revolves around XEROX copy machines mysteriously changing numbers on scanned documents. David goes to the bottom of it and discovers that the problem is caused by pattern matching of JBig2 image compression algorithm. Dangerous little error, causing thousands of scanned documents to be infested with wrong data. It got me thinking about optical character recognition systems, which in some sense is also a scanning process. Normally, one would want an OCR system to recognise the text perfectly. Imagine, how great it can be — snap a photo or screenshot and just copy the text on the picture instead of tedious typing down the text from the picture. But what if I don’t want my text to be recognisable? Can I have a text, legible and/or readable for humans, but problematic for OCR software?

Type design has been on my radar for some time, I even extended the character sets of some typefaces (for example, if I needed Latin extended characters like ü, å or ø), but I never designed a complete typeface before. Now I wanted to start a series of typographic experiments in order to design type, which is readable only by humans. To limit my experiments, I decided to only use traditional graphic designer “weapons” — space and negative space, along with OpenType features, which give me control over glyph position and substitution and Variable Font technology.

Outline

As a first step, I needed to figure out some strategy. After extensive reading on how OCR works, testing some typefaces with an OCR system of my choice, and asking questions about how we humans read and how our brain helps us find patterns and address the information deficiencies, three strategies have been developed. The first one plays with the human capability of recognising the letter shape, the second uses our ability to fill the gaps and the third one employs the ability of spatial reasoning.

For each strategy I decided to draw a basic set of glyphs A-Z, a-z and 0-9, leaving out punctuation, symbols, and marks. For the resulting three typefaces, I conducted a series of tests to determine how well the text set in this typeface will be recognised by Tesseract, the OCR of my choice. Testing my three typefaces — Resolution Display VF, Disruption Monospace VF and Disorientation Sans VF brought some interesting and sometimes unexpected results as well as raised questions about score reliability of deep learning software.
